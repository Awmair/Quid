Quid reviewed the public website inquiry and tour paths of **25 US senior living communities across nine states** on July 12–13, 2026. We followed only what a family could see publicly. We did not submit forms, pose as a family, or test staff response times.

The clearest finding was not that communities lacked a “Request a Tour” button. Most had a visible next step. The friction appeared **after the click**, when a high-intent visitor reached a form, a general contact route, or a promise that staff would arrange the visit later.

## Direct answer: what happens after “Request a Tour”?

In this targeted field audit, **14 of 25 community websites used an explicit tour, visit, or personal-appointment call to action**. Thirteen of those 14 visible journeys still relied on a form or staff-arranged next step. One visibly launched an automated inquiry journey.

Across the full sample, 20 of 25 observations showed a public signal of manual or delayed coordination: a form without visible scheduling, a general contact route, or language saying a person would arrange the visit later.

This does **not** mean 80% of all US senior living communities use manual tour coordination. The sample was intentionally assembled while researching independent communities and small operators with possible inquiry-to-tour friction. It is a field audit of that targeted sample, not a randomized industry prevalence study.

## The button was rarely the problem

The public-facing call to action was usually clear enough: schedule a tour, request a tour, schedule a visit, or contact the community. The hidden cost appeared in the handoff between the website and the admissions team.

  <article><span>1</span><strong>Family clicks</strong><small>“Request a Tour” signals high intent.</small></article>
  <i aria-hidden="true">→</i>
  <article class="audit-flow-friction"><span>2</span><strong>Form creates a pause</strong><small>No time is selected and ownership is not visible.</small></article>
  <i aria-hidden="true">→</i>
  <article><span>3</span><strong>Staff coordinates later</strong><small>Someone must notice, reply, and compare calendars.</small></article>
</div>

That does not prove a community responds slowly. A team may have an excellent internal process. It does show that the visitor cannot see the tour being secured at the moment of highest intent.

## What “manual or delayed coordination” means here

We classified a visible journey as manual or delayed when the public page did at least one of the following:

- collected contact details without exposing tour availability;
- said admissions or another staff member would contact the family to arrange the visit;
- routed a tour or appointment promise into a general contact form;
- used a “Request Information” path with a tour-interest checkbox; or
- displayed a tour form that was not accepting responses.

We did not inspect a community’s CRM, inbox rules, staffing coverage, response time, or conversion rate. The audit measures the **visible website journey**, not internal performance.

## Why the gap matters commercially

A tour request is not an ordinary contact message. It is a family asking to take the next buying step. When the website captures intent but cannot complete or clearly advance that step, the community creates work for staff and uncertainty for the family at the same time.

The practical fix is not to remove people from admissions. It is to automate the predictable coordination around them:

1. acknowledge that the request arrived;
2. offer only approved tour availability;
3. confirm the selected visit;
4. notify the responsible staff member;
5. pause when the family asks a sensitive or complex question; and
6. surface anything stalled for daily review.

## Methodology

This first edition uses 25 fully evaluated rows from Quid’s outreach research completed on July 12–13, 2026.

- **Population reviewed:** US communities publicly presenting assisted living, memory care, personal care, or related senior living options.
- **Operator focus:** independent communities and small operators of roughly one to 10 locations.
- **Geography:** nine states in the initial research wave.
- **Unit of analysis:** one public community website journey. Three records belong to different locations of the same seven-location operator.
- **Evidence:** the visible CTA, inquiry or tour page, and the next step described on the public website.
- **Safety:** no form submission, impersonation, resident information, medical information, or private data collection.
- **Limitations:** targeted convenience sample, small sample size, public website view only, and no measurement of actual response time or conversion.

## What senior living teams can check today

Open your own website on a phone and follow the same path without using staff knowledge to fill in the gaps.

- Can a family tell that the request arrived?
- Can they select a real tour time, or only ask to be contacted?
- Is the owner of the next step visible internally?
- What happens after hours or on weekends?
- Does follow-up stop when the family replies?
- Can clinical, eligibility, medication, pricing-exception, or sensitive questions reach a person immediately?
- Can a manager see tomorrow which requests are still stuck?
